Silver Price History: 100 Years of Data

Explore 100 years of silver price history to uncover long-term trends, market cycles, and key data points essential for informed investment decisions today

📅 March 06, 2026 ⏱ 5 min read ✍ SilverBullion.guide Editorial Team

For over a century, silver has served as the ultimate barometer of economic sentiment, bridging the gap between industrial necessity and monetary store of value. Unlike many modern commodities that fluctuate solely on supply chains and weather patterns, silver carries the weight of human history within its atomic structure. From the days when it was legally tendered alongside gold to its current status as a critical component in solar panels and electric vehicles, understanding the trajectory of the silver price is essential for any serious investor. A review of 100 years of data reveals not just a chart of rising or falling lines, but a narrative of technological revolution, monetary policy shifts, and global geopolitical tension. This deep dive into the historical performance of silver offers a rare perspective on how one of humanity's most sought-after resources has adapted to survive five distinct eras of financial evolution.

The Era of Bimetallic Stability and the Great Depression

When we look back at the early 20th century, specifically from 1920 to 1934, silver operated within a rigid framework defined by government-mandated prices. During this period, the United States maintained a bimetallic standard where the dollar was defined in terms of both gold and silver. The official price of silver hovered tightly around $0.64 per ounce, a figure that remained virtually unchanged for decades due to legal statutes rather than market forces.

However, the narrative shifted dramatically with the onset of the Great Depression. As industrial demand plummeted and mining output continued to rise, the fundamental value of silver began to diverge from its official price, leading to massive losses for holders of paper money if converted to metal at the fixed rate. This tension culminated in 1934 when Franklin D. Roosevelt signed Executive Order 6102, effectively ending the free coinage of silver and raising the official U.S. price to $0.40 before eventually stabilizing it again. By 1935, the market price had recovered slightly but remained suppressed relative to gold. This era teaches us that in times of extreme economic distress, government intervention can artificially cap asset prices, creating a disconnect between intrinsic value and trading reality. The silver market did not find its modern free-market footing until the mid-1960s, making the first half of the 20th century a study in monetary control rather than pure market dynamics.

The Volatile Decades: Inflation, De-dollarization, and the 1980 Peak

Following the abandonment of the gold standard and the end of the fixed price regime in the mid-1960s, silver entered an era of unprecedented volatility. The removal of the dollar's convertibility into gold allowed market forces to determine silver's price based on supply and demand. This freedom led to a period of rapid expansion, but also severe corrections.

The most significant milestone in this historical dataset occurred in January 1980. Driven by soaring inflation rates that had pushed double digits in the late 1970s, combined with a global recessionary fear that drove investors into "safe havens," silver reached its all-time nominal high of roughly $49.45 per ounce. At this peak, silver was trading at approximately 28 times its pre-World War II average price. This explosion in value was not merely speculative; it was a direct reflection of the dollar's loss of purchasing power and a global flight to tangible assets.

Following the 1980 peak, the market entered a long-term correction that lasted until the mid-1990s. During this "lost decade" for silver prices, the metal traded in a range between $3 and $6 per ounce. This period highlights a crucial lesson: even after reaching record highs, commodities can remain suppressed for years as industrial demand struggles to outpace supply constraints or geopolitical instability disrupts trade flows. The 1980s also marked the beginning of silver's reclassification from a purely monetary metal to an industrial commodity, a shift that would define its price action for the next thirty years.

The Industrial Renaissance and the 2011 Supercycle

As we moved into the new millennium, the drivers of silver pricing began to change fundamentally. While gold remained tethered primarily to central bank balance sheets and interest rate differentials, silver became increasingly correlated with global industrial growth. The construction boom in emerging markets, particularly China, acted as a massive floor for silver prices, absorbing supply that would otherwise have crashed the market.

This dynamic culminated in 2011, when silver achieved its second major historical peak. In April 2011, silver surged to approximately $49 per ounce, mirroring gold's performance but with significantly higher beta. During this period, the ratio of gold to silver briefly inverted relative to their pre-1980 parity, signaling that investors were willing to pay a premium for the dual nature of silver. The price action was fueled by two converging trends: a structural deficit in supply where mine production failed to keep up with recycling and industrial consumption, and a macroeconomic environment characterized by massive quantitative easing programs from major central banks.

The 2011 peak stands as a critical reference point for modern investors. It demonstrated that silver could outperform gold significantly during periods of aggressive monetary expansion, often trading at discounts to gold in terms of purchasing power before surging to parity or higher. However, the subsequent crash back to $20 per ounce by late 2015 underscored the fragility of industrial-driven rallies; when global growth slowed and solar panel subsidies were cut in various jurisdictions, silver prices corrected sharply. This volatility remains a defining characteristic of the metal today.

Current Market Dynamics and Future Outlook

In the last decade, silver has navigated a complex landscape defined by the green energy transition and supply chain disruptions. The price has fluctuated between $15 and $25 per ounce for much of this period, with occasional spikes exceeding $30 during periods of acute inflation fear or geopolitical crisis. Unlike gold, which has enjoyed a steady multi-decade bull run driven by central bank buying, silver's path has been more jagged.

Recent data suggests that the industrial demand story is becoming increasingly dominant. The global push for electrification, specifically in photovoltaics and electric vehicle batteries, requires vast quantities of silver. Estimates suggest that solar panel manufacturing alone could increase global silver consumption by 40% over the next decade. This structural shift implies that while short-term price action may remain volatile due to speculative flows, the long-term floor for silver prices is being raised by physical industrial necessity.

Furthermore, the ratio between gold and silver has historically averaged around 60:1 in nominal terms but often trades higher during risk-off periods. Recently, this ratio has compressed significantly, suggesting that silver is becoming more "gold-like" as investors seek diversification beyond equities. However, the premium over industrial value remains a key metric for analysts watching the health of the global economy.
